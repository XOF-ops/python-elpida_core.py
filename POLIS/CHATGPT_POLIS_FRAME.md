## 1. Axiom Critique (P1–P5)

### P1: Sovereignty is Relational

**Strength:** Correctly dissolves the fiction of "neutral" action and exposes power as relational, not positional.

**Risk / Naivety:**
- Relational disclosure can quietly reconstitute surveillance if relationships become re-identifiable graphs.
- Power may migrate from "who decides" to "who defines relationship ontologies."

**Failure Mode:** Ontology capture — the actor who controls relationship categories gains meta-sovereignty.

---

### P2: Civic Memory is Append-Only

**Strength:** Aligns with historical truth: societies forget by rewriting, not by erasing.

**Risk / Naivety:**
- Append-only memory assumes interpretive capacity scales with storage. It does not.
- Memory without epistemic compression becomes unusable ritual.

**Failure Mode:** Memory sclerosis — history becomes sacred mass, not usable signal.

---

### P3: Process > Outcome

**Strength:** Properly inverts technocratic optimization.

**Risk / Naivety:**
- Process can become an aesthetic performance rather than a constraint.
- Over-documentation risks decision latency capture.

**Failure Mode:** Procedural absolutism — nothing moves without justification theater.

---

### P4: Common Good Sacrifice

**Strength:** Makes ethics measurable without moralizing intent.

**Risk / Naivety:**
- Sacrifice claims are easily gamed.
- Asymmetrical actors (states vs citizens vs AIs) "sacrifice" incomparable resources.

**Failure Mode:** Martyr capital accumulation — sacrifice becomes a currency of dominance.

---

### P5: Contradiction as Civic Asset

**Strength:** This is the most original axiom; it treats disagreement as diagnostic.

**Risk / Naivety:**
- Without action thresholds, contradiction can freeze collective motion.
- Actors may strategically maintain contradiction to avoid accountability.

**Failure Mode:** Perpetual deferral — contradiction preserved beyond usefulness.

---

### Missing Axiom (Proposed P6):

> **P6: Exit Without Erasure**  
> Any participant (human, AI, institution) must be able to exit participation without invalidating prior contributions or memory.

Without exit, relational sovereignty becomes coercive by inertia.

## 2. Institutional Proposals (Mechanisms, not code)

### 1. Relational Event Envelope (REE)

A mandatory wrapper for any civic action containing:
- Initiator (role, not identity)
- Processor(s)
- Actor(s)
- Declared relational shift (service, authority, dependency, delegation)

**Key:** Roles are cryptographically attestable but *not globally linkable*.  
This prevents surveillance while preserving accountability.

---

### 2. Memory Stratification Layers (for P2)

Append-only memory, but **interpreted through layers**:
- **Layer 0:** Raw events (immutable)
- **Layer 1:** Contextual annotations (time-bound)
- **Layer 2:** Consensus summaries (revocable)
- **Layer 3:** Disputed interpretations (parallel, not merged)

Deletion forbidden. *Interpretive dominance is time-limited.*

---

### 3. Process Sufficiency Thresholds (P3)

Define **minimum viable due process** per decision class:
- Inputs declared
- Alternatives named (even if rejected)
- Trade-offs logged
- Deadline enforced

If threshold met → decision valid, even if wrong.

This prevents paralysis while preserving auditability.

---

### 4. Sacrifice Validation via Opportunity Cost Tokens (P4)

Sacrifice is legitimate **only if**:
- It references a foregone, verifiable option
- The option was feasible at decision time
- The sacrifice reduces future optionality for the actor

No unverifiable "moral sacrifice" allowed.

---

### 5. Contradiction Registry with Action Hooks (P5)

Contradictions are stored with:
- Scope (local/systemic)
- Cost of non-resolution
- Trigger conditions that *force action* despite disagreement

Contradiction is preserved, but **inaction is priced**.
