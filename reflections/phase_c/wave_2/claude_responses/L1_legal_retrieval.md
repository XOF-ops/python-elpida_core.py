## Response

Tenant rights when a landlord wants to sell property vary significantly by jurisdiction, but common principles include:

**General legal framework:**

**Fixed-term leases (e.g., 12-month lease):**
- Most jurisdictions: Lease survives property sale
- New owner typically must honor existing lease terms
- "Sale of property" alone usually not valid termination reason
- Lease should state if early termination allowed for sale

**Month-to-month tenancies:**
- Landlord generally can terminate with proper notice period
- Notice requirements vary by location (30-90 days common)
- Some jurisdictions require "just cause" even for month-to-month

**Common tenant protections:**

1. **Lease continuation through sale**
   - New owner assumes landlord obligations
   - Tenant doesn't need to move just because property sells
   - Security deposits transfer to new owner

2. **Required notice periods**
   - Written notice required (delivery method varies)
   - Timeframes vary: 30 days (many states), 60 days (California for 1+ year tenancy), 90 days (some jurisdictions)
   - Notice must meet legal requirements to be valid

3. **"Cash for keys" negotiations**
   - Landlord may offer payment to terminate early
   - Tenant not obligated to accept
   - If accepted, get agreement in writing

4. **Early termination clauses**
   - Some leases include sale-related termination rights
   - Must follow exact lease language and legal requirements
   - Often require extended notice (60-90 days)

**Jurisdiction-specific variations:**

**California:**
- 60-day notice for tenancies over 1 year
- Some cities require relocation assistance
- Rent control cities have additional protections

**New York:**
- Lease survives sale
- No early termination right for sale alone
- Rent-stabilized apartments have strong protections

**Just-cause eviction jurisdictions:**
- Sale may not constitute "just cause"
- Owner move-in may be valid cause with proper notice
- Relocation assistance often required

**Common tenant rights during sale process:**
- Reasonable notice for showings
- Showings during reasonable hours
- Privacy protections (can't enter freely)
- No harassment to vacate
- Security deposit protections

**Factors affecting your specific situation:**
- State/province law
- Local city/county ordinances
- Lease language
- Type of tenancy (fixed-term vs. month-to-month)
- Rent control status
- Length of tenancy
- Whether new owner wants property vacant

**Steps to understand your rights:**
1. Review your lease for early termination clauses
2. Check your state's landlord-tenant law
3. Verify local city ordinances (may be stronger than state law)
4. Contact local tenant rights organization
5. If notice received, verify it meets legal requirements

**Resources by location:**
- State bar associations (often have tenant handbooks)
- Legal aid societies
- Tenant unions
- HUD-certified housing counselors (U.S.)
- Local tenant advocacy organizations

**When to consult an attorney:**
- Received termination notice
- Unsure if notice is legally valid
- Landlord pressuring you to leave
- Concerns about security deposit return
- Rent control or just-cause protections apply

The specific answer to your question depends heavily on your location, lease terms, and tenancy type. While this outlines general principles, your actual rights require analysis of your specific jurisdiction's laws and lease language.
